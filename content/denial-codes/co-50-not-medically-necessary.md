# CO-50 Insurance Denial: Not Medically Necessary — What It Means and How to Appeal

Fewer than 1% of patients ever appeal a denied insurance claim — but of those who do, 40 to 60% win. If your insurer sent you a denial with code CO-50, you may have more options than you think.

This guide explains exactly what a CO-50 denial means, why it happens, and what steps to take to appeal it effectively.

---

## What Is a CO-50 Denial Code?

CO-50 is a **Claim Adjustment Reason Code (CARC)** issued by insurance companies when they determine that a billed service was not medically necessary under your plan's coverage criteria.

"Medically necessary" is a term insurers define themselves — and it often means something narrower than what your doctor ordered. A CO-50 denial does not mean your doctor made a mistake. It means your insurer decided the service didn't meet their internal criteria for coverage.

CO-50 is one of the most common denial codes in the United States. It affects services ranging from specialist visits and MRI scans to physical therapy, medications, and inpatient stays.

---

## Why Did You Get a CO-50 Denial?

Common reasons a CO-50 denial is issued:

- **Your diagnosis code didn't match** the service billed — insurers check whether the ICD-10 diagnosis code supports the procedure. A mismatch can trigger automatic denial.
- **The service wasn't pre-approved** — some procedures require prior authorization before they are performed. If that step was missed, CO-50 may be applied.
- **The insurer applied a different standard** than your doctor — insurance company clinical criteria often lag behind current medical practice guidelines.
- **A new or experimental treatment** — insurers frequently deny newer treatments as "not medically necessary" even when your doctor considers them the appropriate standard of care.

---

## What Are Your Rights When You Get a CO-50 Denial?

Under federal law governing marketplace and employer-sponsored health plans, you have the right to:

1. **Receive a written explanation** of why your claim was denied and what criteria were used.
2. **Request the insurer's coverage criteria** — specifically, the clinical guidelines they applied to your case.
3. **File an internal appeal** — this is a formal process where your insurer must review the denial again, typically within 30 days (or 72 hours for urgent situations).
4. **Request an Independent Medical Review (IMR)** — if your internal appeal is denied, a neutral third party (not your insurer) must review the decision.

These rights apply to most private health plans. Medicare and Medicaid have similar but separate appeal processes.

## How to Appeal a CO-50 Denial — Step by Step

### Step 1: Get the Denial Letter and Your Explanation of Benefits (EOB)

The denial letter explains the specific reason your claim was denied and the deadline to file an appeal. Your Explanation of Benefits (EOB) shows how the claim was processed. Request both from your insurer if you don't have them.

### Step 2: Request the Insurer's Medical Necessity Criteria

Call your insurer and ask for the clinical coverage guidelines they used to deny your claim. Insurers are required to provide this. Understanding their criteria helps you build a targeted appeal.

### Step 3: Ask Your Doctor for a Letter of Medical Necessity

A **Letter of Medical Necessity** (LMN) from your treating physician is the most important document in a CO-50 appeal. The letter should:

- State your diagnosis and why the denied service is clinically indicated
- Explain what happens if the treatment is not received
- Reference relevant clinical practice guidelines (from specialty societies like the American College of Physicians or the American Cancer Society)
- Address the insurer's stated criteria directly

### Step 4: Write Your Appeal Letter

Your appeal letter should:
- Identify the claim number, date of service, and denial reason (CO-50)
- Include your doctor's Letter of Medical Necessity
- Reference any clinical guidelines that support the treatment
- Be clear, factual, and professional

### Step 5: Submit Before the Deadline

Most plans require appeals within **180 days** of the denial. Read your denial letter carefully — the deadline and submission instructions are included. Submit by certified mail or through the insurer's online portal, and keep a copy of everything.

### Step 6: Request Independent Review If the Internal Appeal Fails

If your insurer upholds the denial after your internal appeal, you can request an **Independent Medical Review (IMR)** through your state insurance department or a federal external review program. An independent reviewer — not connected to your insurer — will make the final determination.

## Tips That Strengthen a CO-50 Appeal

- **Be specific about your diagnosis.** Vague diagnosis codes are easier for insurers to deny. Ask your doctor to use the most specific ICD-10 code.
- **Document what happens without treatment.** If delaying the service creates a safety risk, say so clearly in the appeal.
- **Reference your plan documents.** If your plan's Summary of Benefits and Coverage suggests the service should be covered, quote it.
- **Keep records of every communication.** Note the date, time, and name of every insurer representative you speak with.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**How long does a CO-50 appeal take?**
Most insurers must respond to an internal appeal within 30 days for non-urgent situations, or 72 hours for urgent medical situations.

**Can my doctor appeal on my behalf?**
Yes. In most cases, your provider can file a "provider appeal" on your behalf. Ask your doctor's billing office whether they will do this.

**What if I already received the service and am now being billed?**
A CO-50 denial after the fact still follows the same appeal process. You can still dispute it within your plan's appeal window.

**What is an Independent Medical Review?**
An IMR (also called an External Appeal) brings in a reviewer who is not affiliated with your insurer. They evaluate whether the denial was medically appropriate. Their decision is typically binding on the insurer.
