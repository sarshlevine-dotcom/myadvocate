# CO-97 Denial Code: Bundled Billing — What It Means and How to Dispute It

A CO-97 code on your Explanation of Benefits can be confusing. You received two separate services, but the insurer says one is already included in the other's payment. Here's what that actually means — and what to do when it's wrong.

---

## What Is a CO-97 Denial Code?

CO-97 is a Claim Adjustment Reason Code that means:

> "The benefit for this service is included in the payment/allowance for another service/procedure that has already been adjudicated."

In plain terms: your insurer believes the billed service was already bundled into the payment for a different procedure. Rather than paying separately, they applied a bundling rule and denied the secondary service.

CO-97 is one of the most frequent administrative denial codes, particularly for outpatient procedures, lab work performed during surgery, and multi-step treatments billed on the same date.

---

## Why Do Insurers Apply CO-97?

Most CO-97 denials trace back to the **National Correct Coding Initiative (NCCI)** — a CMS program that identifies pairs of procedure codes that are typically performed together and should be billed as one unit rather than two.

When a provider bills both procedures separately and the payer detects an NCCI pair, CO-97 is applied automatically.

This isn't always wrong. NCCI bundling rules exist because some procedures genuinely include a component service — there's no separate work performed. However, the rules also get applied incorrectly when services were truly distinct.

---

## When Is a CO-97 Denial Disputable?

A CO-97 denial may be overturned when:

**1. The services were performed on different dates**
NCCI edits apply to same-date billing. If the two procedures occurred on separate days, the bundling rule shouldn't apply.

**2. A modifier applies**
Modifiers signal to the payer that bundling rules shouldn't apply in this case. The most common:
- **Modifier -59** (Distinct Procedural Service): signals the second service was a separate and distinct procedure
- **Modifier -XS, -XU, -XE, -XP** (granular X-modifiers): more specific alternatives to -59 that identify why the services were distinct

If your provider billed without the appropriate modifier and the services were genuinely separate, the provider can submit a corrected claim with the modifier added.

**3. The NCCI edit doesn't apply to your plan**
NCCI rules govern Medicare billing. Commercial insurers may adopt them, adapt them, or use their own bundling logic. If a CO-97 denial comes from a commercial plan and the cited procedure pair isn't actually on the NCCI bundling list for that plan, you have grounds to dispute.

---

## How to Dispute a CO-97 Denial

### Step 1: Request Your Explanation of Benefits (EOB)

Your EOB shows which procedures were bundled and which was denied. Get the specific procedure codes (CPT codes) involved.

### Step 2: Check Whether a Modifier Applies

Ask your provider's billing department to review the CPT codes and confirm whether a -59 or XS modifier should have been included. Billing errors are common — a corrected claim with the right modifier often resolves CO-97 quickly.

### Step 3: Verify the Dates of Service

If the two procedures occurred on different days, the provider should submit a corrected claim noting the correct dates. A CO-97 applied to different-date procedures is a processing error.

### Step 4: File a Formal Appeal if Needed

If the provider has confirmed that the bundling was incorrect but the insurer won't reverse it after a corrected claim, file a formal appeal. Include:
- The EOB showing the denial
- Documentation that the services were distinct (operative notes, clinical records)
- A written explanation from your provider supporting the claim

## Frequently Asked Questions

**My provider says they'll handle it — do I need to do anything?**
If the provider's billing office is actively correcting the claim, they usually handle the process. Keep copies of the denial and follow up in two weeks if you haven't heard.

**Will I owe money if CO-97 isn't reversed?**
That depends on your agreement with your provider. For in-network providers, CO-97 typically results in the provider absorbing the cost — not billing you. Confirm this with your provider's billing office.

**Can I appeal CO-97 myself?**
Yes. As the patient, you have the right to file an appeal with your insurer regardless of whether your provider is also appealing.
