# PR-204 Denial Code: Service Not Covered Under Your Plan — What to Do

A PR-204 denial often feels like a dead end. Your insurer says the service simply isn't covered — and there's nothing more to say. But that's not always the full story. Here's what PR-204 actually means, when it can be challenged, and what options remain even when coverage is genuinely excluded.

---

## What Is a PR-204 Denial Code?

PR-204 is a Claim Adjustment Reason Code meaning:

> "This service/equipment/drug is not covered by the plan."

It is a **patient-responsibility code** in the PR (Patient Responsibility) series, which means the insurer is indicating that no insurance benefit applies and the financial responsibility may fall to you.

PR-204 is common for services that your plan explicitly excludes from coverage — things like certain cosmetic procedures, some forms of alternative medicine, or services that require plan-specific prior approval your plan doesn't offer.

---

## Not All PR-204 Denials Are Correct

Before accepting a PR-204 denial, it's worth verifying a few things:

### 1. Was the service coded correctly?

Billing codes matter. If your provider billed a service under the wrong procedure code (CPT code), the insurer may have matched it to an excluded service when the actual treatment should have been covered. Ask your provider for the specific procedure code billed and compare it to your plan's coverage documents.

### 2. Is the service listed as excluded in your plan documents?

Your **Summary of Benefits and Coverage (SBC)** and plan documents include an explicit list of excluded services. Pull up your SBC (available from your insurer or employer's HR department) and look up the specific service. If it's not on the exclusion list, the PR-204 may have been applied incorrectly.

### 3. Was the service denied as "not covered" when it should have been reviewed for medical necessity?

Sometimes a CO-50 (not medically necessary) denial gets coded as PR-204 (not covered). These are different — CO-50 can be appealed on clinical grounds, while PR-204 involves a plan exclusion. If you believe the denial was about medical necessity rather than a categorical exclusion, file an appeal requesting the distinction be clarified.

## What Are Your Options After a PR-204 Denial?

### Option 1: Appeal the Denial

Even if a service is listed as excluded, you can appeal on the grounds that:
- The service was coded under the wrong procedure code
- The exclusion was applied incorrectly based on your plan's actual terms
- An exception should be granted because no covered alternative exists for your condition

Your insurer must provide an appeal process for any adverse benefit determination.

### Option 2: Request a Coverage Exception

Some plans allow coverage exceptions for medically necessary services when there is no covered alternative. Ask your plan directly whether an exception process exists. Your doctor will need to submit documentation supporting the exception request.

### Option 3: Check Whether the Service Is Required Under Federal Rules

Certain preventive services, mental health services, and essential health benefits are required to be covered under federal guidelines for marketplace and most employer-sponsored plans — even if your plan's documents suggest otherwise. If your PR-204 involves one of these categories, the exclusion may not be enforceable.

## How to File an Appeal for a PR-204 Denial

1. **Get your denial letter and EOB.** These contain the claim number, denial reason, and appeal deadline.
2. **Request your Summary of Benefits and Coverage.** Check whether the service is actually listed as excluded.
3. **Contact your provider's billing office.** Ask them to confirm the procedure code billed and whether a correction is appropriate.
4. **Write your appeal letter.** Explain why the PR-204 was applied incorrectly, or why an exception should apply. Attach supporting documentation.
5. **Submit before the deadline.** Most plans require appeals within 180 days of the denial.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**If the service truly isn't covered, do I have to pay the full billed amount?**
Not necessarily. For in-network providers, you typically owe the "allowed amount" under the contract, not the billed charge. Ask your provider what you owe and whether any financial assistance programs are available.

**What if PR-204 was applied after I already received the service?**
You can still appeal a PR-204 denial that comes after the fact, within your plan's appeal window. The process is the same.

**Can my doctor appeal on my behalf?**
Yes. Providers can submit appeals and corrected claims on the patient's behalf. Ask your provider's billing office to review the claim.
