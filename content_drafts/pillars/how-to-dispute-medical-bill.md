# How to Dispute a Medical Bill: Template Letter and Negotiation Script

Fewer than 1% of patients ever challenge a medical bill — even though billing errors and administrative mistakes are the single largest category of insurance denials, accounting for roughly 50–60% of all rejected claims (KFF analysis of Healthcare.gov data through 2023). Most of those errors are fixable without a formal appeal. You just need to know how to ask.

This guide covers how to dispute a medical bill, whether the issue is an insurance denial, a billing error, or a charge that doesn't match what you were told you'd owe.

---

## Medical Bill vs. Insurance Denial: Which Problem Do You Have?

Before disputing, it helps to identify exactly what went wrong. There are two different situations:

**Your insurance denied the claim.** The insurer rejected the claim entirely or paid less than expected. This shows up on your Explanation of Benefits (EOB) as a denial. You'll also receive a denial letter explaining the reason code.

**There's a billing error or unexpected charge.** The insurer may have processed the claim, but the amount billed doesn't look right — duplicate charges, wrong codes, a charge for a service you didn't receive, or a bill that doesn't match your cost estimate.

Both situations can be disputed. The approach is slightly different for each.

If you received a formal denial letter, the [full insurance denial appeal guide](/how-to-appeal-insurance-denial) covers the complete process. This guide focuses on billing disputes — the paperwork and billing errors that account for the majority of problems.

---

## Common Medical Billing Errors

These are the most frequently seen billing mistakes and what they mean for your dispute:

**Duplicate billing** — the same service was billed twice. Look for identical dates, procedure codes, and amounts on your EOB. This is a straightforward correction that your provider's billing office can fix.

**Incorrect procedure code** — the wrong CPT code was used, either over-billing or under-billing for what was actually done. This often results in a denial because the code doesn't match the diagnosis or doesn't meet coverage criteria.

**Unbundling** — services that should be billed together as one code are billed separately to increase the total charge. The denial code for this is usually [CO-97](/denial-codes/co-97-bundled-included-service).

**Missing or invalid information** — the claim was denied because required information was missing. This is the most common denial code ([CO-16](/denial-codes/co-16-missing-invalid-information)), and it's almost always fixable with a corrected resubmission from your provider.

**Charge exceeds the fee schedule** — the provider billed above the contracted rate with your insurer. The denial code is [CO-45](/denial-codes/co-45-charge-exceeds-fee-schedule). This is typically a provider billing error, not a patient action item, but you should confirm you're not being balance-billed improperly.

**Balance billing for network services** — you were billed for the difference between a provider's charge and the insurer's allowed amount, even though both parties are in-network. This may violate your state's balance billing protections.

## What the No Surprises Act Covers

The No Surprises Act, which took effect in 2022, provides federal protections against surprise medical bills in specific situations:

- Emergency services at any hospital, regardless of whether it's in-network
- Non-emergency services at an in-network facility when you didn't have a reasonable choice about which provider treated you
- Air ambulance services from certain providers

Under the No Surprises Act, you generally cannot be billed more than your in-network cost-sharing amount for services covered by the act. If you received an emergency bill that appears to violate these protections, your dispute has a strong statutory basis.

State laws may provide broader protections than the federal law. Contact your state insurance commissioner or check state-specific guidance for what applies in your situation.

## Step 1 — Get an Itemized Bill

Request an itemized bill from your provider if you don't already have one. This is a line-by-line breakdown of every charge, including the procedure codes, dates of service, and amounts billed. Providers are required to give you this upon request.

Compare the itemized bill to:
- Your Explanation of Benefits (EOB) from your insurer
- Any cost estimates you received before the service
- Your insurance plan's Summary of Benefits and Coverage (SBC)

Discrepancies between any of these documents are your starting point for a dispute.

---

## Step 2 — Identify the Specific Error

Be as specific as possible before contacting anyone. Know:

- Which line item or charge you're disputing
- What you believe the correct charge should be
- The reason for the discrepancy (duplicate, wrong code, exceeds fee schedule, etc.)
- Any documentation you have that supports your position

Specific disputes get resolved faster than general complaints. "Line 4, CPT code 99213, was billed twice on the same date" is more actionable than "the bill seems too high."

---

## Step 3 — Contact the Billing Department First

Most billing disputes can be resolved directly with your provider's billing office before involving your insurer. Call or write to:

- Request a review of the specific charge you've identified
- Ask them to verify the procedure code against your medical records
- Ask for a corrected claim to be resubmitted to your insurer if a code was wrong

Keep notes of every call: date, time, name of the person you spoke with, and what they said. This record protects you if you need to escalate.

---

## Step 4 — Write a Formal Dispute Letter

If the billing office doesn't resolve the issue, put your dispute in writing. A formal dispute letter:

- Identifies you by name, account number, and date of service
- Specifies the exact charge being disputed
- States clearly what you believe is wrong and why
- Lists all supporting documentation attached
- Requests a specific resolution (corrected claim, adjusted bill, or written explanation)
- Sets a deadline for response (30 days is standard)

Send the letter by certified mail to both your provider's billing office and, if the insurer processed the claim incorrectly, to your insurer's member services address.

## Step 5 — Negotiate If the Bill Is Correct but Unaffordable

If the bill is accurate but the amount is more than you can pay, you have options:

**Request a financial hardship review.** Most hospitals and large provider groups have financial assistance programs (charity care) for patients who can't afford their bills. Ask for a financial assistance application — you don't need to be in extreme poverty to qualify.

**Request a payment plan.** Providers typically offer extended payment plans. Ask specifically about interest-free options. Many will negotiate on the total amount in exchange for a lump-sum payment.

**Ask about prompt-pay discounts.** Providers sometimes reduce bills by 10–30% for patients who can pay the full amount quickly.

**Negotiate the rate directly.** If you're uninsured or your insurer didn't cover the service, ask the billing department what the Medicare-equivalent rate is for the procedure. This is a reasonable starting point for negotiation. Providers regularly accept less than the sticker price.

Document any agreement in writing before making any payment.

---

## When to Escalate

If the billing office and your insurer don't resolve your dispute:

- File a complaint with your **state insurance commissioner** if the insurer processed the claim incorrectly or violated your plan terms
- Contact your state's **consumer assistance program** for help navigating disputes
- For bills involving the No Surprises Act, file a complaint through **cms.gov** (the federal portal for No Surprises Act violations)
- For Medicare billing disputes, contact the **Medicare Administrative Contractor (MAC)** that handles your region

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Can a medical bill go to collections while I'm disputing it?**
You have the right to dispute a bill before it's sent to collections. Notify the provider and insurer in writing that the bill is under dispute. Under the No Surprises Act, surprise bills cannot go to collections during a dispute. For other bills, put your dispute in writing immediately to establish a formal record.

**How long do I have to dispute a medical bill?**
There's no universal federal deadline, but most plans have internal dispute deadlines, and providers typically send bills to collections after 90–180 days. Act quickly. If you receive a bill you disagree with, request an itemized statement the same week.

**What if the billing error is the provider's fault?**
Most billing errors are on the provider side — wrong codes, duplicates, unbundled services. It's still your responsibility to catch them. Providers are legally required to cooperate with correction requests.

**Do I need to pay the bill while disputing it?**
For disputed charges, you can often withhold the disputed portion while paying the undisputed portions to show good faith. State laws vary. Put your dispute in writing before withholding any payment.
