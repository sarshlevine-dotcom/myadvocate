# How to Appeal an Insurance Denial: Complete Step-by-Step Guide

Fewer than 1% of patients ever appeal an insurance denial — even though 40–60% of those who do, win. According to KFF analysis of Healthcare.gov data through 2023, that gap isn't because appeals don't work. It's because insurance companies have systems, and most people don't know where to start.

This guide gives you the process, the language, and the next steps to push back.

---

## What Is an Insurance Denial Appeal?

An insurance denial appeal is a formal request to your insurer to reconsider a claim they've rejected. You have the legal right to appeal most denials. The Affordable Care Act requires insurers to provide a full appeals process, including access to independent external review if your internal appeal fails.

You don't need a lawyer to file an appeal. Most successful appeals are filed by patients and family members who understand the process.

---

## Why Insurance Denials Happen

About 1 in 5 insurance claims are denied. The reasons fall into three broad categories:

**Administrative and documentation errors** account for roughly 50–60% of all denials. These are billing mistakes, missing information, incorrect codes, or paperwork problems. They often don't require a formal appeal — just a corrected resubmission. If your denial is in this category, the [Medical Bill Dispute tool](/how-to-dispute-medical-bill) is your fastest path.

**Medical necessity denials** account for roughly 25–35% of denials. Your insurer decided the service wasn't medically necessary under their coverage criteria. These require a formal written appeal backed by clinical documentation. See the full guide to [medical necessity denials](/insurance-denial/medical-necessity-denial).

**Coverage and network issues** account for roughly 10–15% of denials. The service or provider isn't covered under your plan, or is out of network. The No Surprises Act provides protections for emergency situations. See the guide to [coverage and network denials](/insurance-denial/coverage-network-denial).

Understanding which category your denial falls into tells you immediately which path to take and which tool will help you most.

## Step 1 — Read the Denial Letter Carefully

Before you do anything else, read the denial letter from start to finish. It contains everything you need to begin your appeal:

- The **denial reason code** (such as CO-50 or CO-16) — this is the specific reason your insurer cited
- The **appeal deadline** — most insurers give you 30 to 180 days; missing this date ends your right to appeal
- The **internal appeal address** or instructions for submitting online
- Your right to request a copy of the insurer's coverage criteria

If the denial letter is hard to understand, the [How to Read an Insurance Denial Letter](/how-to-read-insurance-denial-letter) guide breaks down every section in plain language.

## Step 2 — Look Up Your Denial Code

The denial reason code on your letter is not random. Every code has a specific meaning and a specific fix.

The most common codes are:

- **[CO-16](/denial-codes/co-16-missing-invalid-information)** — Missing or invalid information. This is a paperwork issue. Routes to a billing correction, not a formal appeal.
- **[CO-50](/denial-codes/co-50-not-medically-necessary)** — Not medically necessary. This requires a formal written appeal with clinical documentation.
- **CO-22** — Coordination of benefits. You may have two insurance plans and the wrong one was billed first.
- **CO-29** — Timely filing. The claim was submitted after the deadline. Note that this is the provider's deadline, not necessarily yours as a patient.

## Step 3 — Gather Your Documentation

The strength of an appeal depends on what you submit with it. The documents you need depend on why the claim was denied.

**For medical necessity denials:**
- A letter from your treating physician explaining why the service was medically necessary
- Clinical notes from office visits related to the service
- Any peer-reviewed literature or treatment guidelines supporting the recommended care
- Your prior treatment history showing what else was tried before this service

**For administrative and billing denials:**
- The original Explanation of Benefits (EOB)
- A corrected claim or updated documentation from your provider's billing office
- Any prior authorization numbers or referral documentation

**For coverage disputes:**
- A copy of your Summary of Benefits and Coverage (SBC)
- Documentation of any emergency situation if the No Surprises Act applies
- State insurance commissioner guidance if your state has stronger protections than federal law

Request copies of your medical records if you don't already have them. Under HIPAA, your provider must provide them within 30 days.

---

## Step 4 — Write Your Appeal Letter

Your appeal letter does three things: identifies the denial clearly, states your grounds for appeal, and tells the insurer exactly what you want them to do.

A strong appeal letter:

- References the specific claim number, date of service, and denial code
- States the grounds for appeal plainly — either the denial reason is incorrect, or the clinical criteria are met and can be documented
- Attaches all supporting documentation
- Cites the specific coverage provision or clinical guideline at issue, where applicable
- Requests a response within the required timeframe

What a strong appeal letter does not do: predict outcomes, threaten legal action, or make claims it cannot back up with documentation.

## Step 5 — Submit the Appeal

Submit your appeal within the deadline stated on your denial letter. Keep a copy of everything you submit. If you send by mail, use certified mail with return receipt.

**Request an expedited appeal if:**
- Your health condition is serious or ongoing
- A delay would cause significant harm
- Your doctor certifies urgency

Under ACA rules, insurers must respond to expedited appeals within 72 hours. Standard internal appeals must receive a response within 30 to 60 days depending on the type of claim.

---

## Step 6 — If the Internal Appeal Is Denied

If your insurer denies your internal appeal, you have the right to an **external review** by an independent organization. This is a federal right under the ACA for most plans, and most states have their own external review laws that go further.

External reviewers are independent of your insurer. For medical necessity denials, external reviews overturn the insurer's decision in roughly 40–60% of cases, according to HHS Office of Inspector General data.

**If external review fails**, additional options include:
- Filing a complaint with your state insurance commissioner
- Requesting an ombudsman review through your state's consumer assistance program
- For Medicare enrollees, pursuing the Medicare appeals process through ALJ hearings, which overturn roughly 60% of cases at that stage (HHS OIG data)

## Common Mistakes That Sink Appeals

**Missing the deadline.** The most common reason appeals fail is that they're filed late. Find the deadline on your denial letter the day it arrives.

**Submitting without documentation.** An appeal letter alone is rarely enough for a medical necessity denial. Your physician's supporting letter is critical.

**Using the wrong appeal path.** A billing error (CO-16, CO-97, CO-45) doesn't need a formal appeal — it needs a corrected resubmission. Using the formal appeal process for a billing fix wastes time and signals the wrong thing to the insurer.

**Accepting the first denial.** Most people stop here. The data says most people who push back, win.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**How long do I have to appeal an insurance denial?**
Most plans give you between 30 and 180 days from the denial date. The deadline is printed on your denial letter. Missing it means losing your right to appeal that claim. Act immediately.

**Do I need a lawyer to appeal an insurance denial?**
No. Most successful appeals are filed by patients or family members without legal representation. MyAdvocate gives you the tools to do it yourself. If your appeal fails through all internal and external review channels, and the denial involves a significant amount, speaking with a patient advocate or attorney becomes an option — but that's a last step, not the first one.

**What is the success rate for insurance appeals?**
According to KFF analysis of Healthcare.gov data through 2023, 40–60% of appeals result in the denial being overturned when patients follow through. The success rate at independent external review and Medicare ALJ hearings is similar or higher.

**Can my employer help with an appeal?**
If your insurance is through an employer, your HR or benefits administrator can sometimes intervene with the insurer directly. Larger employers often have dedicated benefits advocates. Ask your HR department.

**What if I can't afford to wait for the appeal?**
Request an expedited appeal. If your doctor can certify that a delay would cause significant harm to your health, your insurer must respond within 72 hours. In emergencies, your state insurance commissioner can also intervene faster than the standard appeals timeline.
