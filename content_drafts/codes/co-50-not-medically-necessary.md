# How to Appeal a Medical Necessity Denial (CO-50): Step-by-Step Guide

A CO-50 denial means your insurance company decided your treatment wasn't "medically necessary." It's one of the most frustrating denials you can receive — especially when your doctor ordered the treatment because they believed you needed it.

The good news: fewer than 1% of patients ever appeal an insurance denial — even though 40–60% of those who do, win, according to KFF analysis of Healthcare.gov data through 2023. A CO-50 denial is not the end of the road. It is a starting point.

## What Does CO-50 Mean?

CO-50 is a standardized ANSI denial code used by insurers and claims processors. It stands for: "the payer determined that this service is not medically necessary."

In plain language, your insurer reviewed the claim and decided the treatment, procedure, or medication didn't meet their internal standards for what qualifies as necessary care. This doesn't mean your doctor was wrong. It means your insurer needs more information — or their criteria is different from your doctor's judgment.

## Why Do CO-50 Denials Happen?

CO-50 denials can feel like your insurer is second-guessing your doctor. Here are the most common reasons they happen:

- **Missing or incomplete clinical documentation.** The insurer received the billing code but didn't receive the clinical notes that justify the treatment.
- **Failed prior authorization.** The procedure required pre-approval, but the approval wasn't obtained — or wasn't obtained for the right diagnosis code.
- **Treatment doesn't match the insurer's coverage criteria.** Some insurers use their own clinical criteria (like InterQual or MCG) that differ from standard medical guidelines.
- **Wrong diagnosis code linked to the procedure.** If the diagnosis doesn't match the treatment in the insurer's system, it may auto-deny.
- **Experimental or off-label use.** Treatments used outside their FDA-approved indication are frequently flagged.

## How to Fix a CO-50 Denial: Step-by-Step

### Step 1: Get the denial letter and read it carefully

Your insurer is required to send you a written denial explaining which clinical criteria they used and why your treatment didn't meet it. Find the specific criterion they cited — this is what your appeal needs to address directly.

### Step 2: Call your doctor's office

Ask your doctor to write a Letter of Medical Necessity (LMN). This letter should explain why the treatment is medically necessary for your specific condition, cite peer-reviewed clinical guidelines, and connect your diagnosis directly to the treatment ordered.

### Step 3: Gather supporting documentation

Pull together your medical records, prior test results, previous treatments tried, and any peer-reviewed studies supporting the treatment. The stronger your clinical record, the harder it is for the insurer to maintain the denial.

### Step 4: Write your appeal letter

Your appeal should do three things: (1) directly address the denial criteria the insurer cited, (2) include your doctor's Letter of Medical Necessity, and (3) cite the clinical evidence backing your case. Keep it factual and focused.

### Step 5: Submit before the deadline

Most insurers require internal appeals within 180 days of the denial date. State laws vary. Check your Explanation of Benefits (EOB) for your specific deadline and instructions.

## When to Appeal vs. When to Resubmit

Not every CO-50 denial requires a full formal appeal. Here's how to tell the difference.

**Resubmit a corrected claim if:** The denial was due to a missing diagnosis code, an incorrect procedure code, or a missing prior authorization that was actually obtained. A corrected claim or a peer-to-peer call from your doctor may resolve it faster than a formal appeal.

**File a formal appeal if:** The insurer reviewed the clinical information and still denied it based on their criteria. This requires you to directly challenge the medical necessity determination with new or stronger evidence.

If the internal appeal is denied, you have the right to request an Independent Medical Review (IMR) — a review by a physician who doesn't work for your insurer. IMR decisions are binding on the insurer in most states.

## Your Rights Under Federal Law

Under the Affordable Care Act (ACA), all health plans must provide at least one level of internal appeal and access to an independent external review. You cannot waive these rights.

CMS and the U.S. Department of Labor publish consumer guidance on appeal rights at [cms.gov](https://www.cms.gov). If your insurer is a state-regulated plan, your state insurance commissioner may also have a formal complaint process that can intervene in certain situations.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**What's the difference between CO-50 and CO-97?**
CO-50 means the insurer said your treatment wasn't medically necessary — a clinical judgment dispute. CO-97 is a billing issue: it means one service is considered "bundled" into a payment already made. They require very different approaches.

**How long does a medical necessity appeal take?**
Internal appeals for urgent care must be decided within 72 hours. For standard care, insurers typically have 30–60 days depending on state law. Check your plan's Summary of Benefits and Coverage (SBC) for your specific timeline.

**Can I appeal without a lawyer?**
Yes. Most successful CO-50 appeals are filed without attorneys. A strong Letter of Medical Necessity from your doctor, paired with relevant clinical guidelines, is typically what wins these cases.

**What is an Independent Medical Review (IMR)?**
An IMR is a review by a board-certified physician with no relationship to your insurer. In most states, if the IMR finds in your favor, the insurer must cover the treatment. Filing an IMR is free — contact your state insurance commissioner or your plan's customer service to request one.

**What if my plan denied the prior authorization before I got the treatment?**
Prior authorization denials follow a similar appeal path. Request a peer-to-peer review between your doctor and the insurer's medical reviewer first — this resolves many prior auth denials before a formal appeal is needed.
