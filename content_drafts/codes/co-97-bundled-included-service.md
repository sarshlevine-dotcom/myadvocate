# CO-97 Denial Code: Bundled or Included Service — How to Dispute a Billing Rollup

Getting a CO-97 denial can be genuinely confusing. Your provider billed for a service, but your insurer says that service is already "included" in another payment. You're not sure who's right — or whether it's worth pushing back.

Fewer than 1% of patients ever appeal an insurance denial — even though 40–60% of those who do, win, according to KFF analysis of Healthcare.gov data through 2023. CO-97 disputes are often resolved with a simple corrected claim, and they're worth pursuing when your provider billed two legitimately separate services.

## What Does CO-97 Mean?

CO-97 is a standardized ANSI denial code. It stands for: "the benefit for this service is included in the payment or allowance for another service or procedure that has already been adjudicated."

In plain language: your insurer believes one of the services billed should be "bundled" into a payment they've already made. They're treating it as a package deal — one payment for multiple services — rather than paying each line item separately.

This can happen correctly (if the services are genuinely bundled under CMS payment rules) or incorrectly (if the services were legitimately distinct and each deserved separate payment).

## Why Do CO-97 Denials Happen?

CO-97 is primarily a billing issue, not a clinical judgment. The most common causes include:

- **CMS bundling rules (NCCI edits).** The National Correct Coding Initiative (NCCI) publishes a list of code pairs that must be billed together. If a provider bills both codes separately, the second one often gets denied as CO-97.
- **Global surgical package.** Many procedures include a "global period" covering pre-op and post-op care. If a provider bills separately for those services, they may be denied as included in the global fee.
- **Missing modifier.** When two procedures are genuinely separate and distinct, a billing modifier (like -59 or -XU) signals to the insurer that the services aren't bundled. Without the correct modifier, the claim may auto-deny.
- **Facility vs. professional billing overlap.** Sometimes a hospital and a physician bill separately for overlapping components of the same visit, triggering a CO-97 on one of the claims.

## Is the Denial Correct? How to Check

Before disputing a CO-97, it's worth verifying whether the denial is legitimate.

**Step 1:** Get your Explanation of Benefits (EOB). It will show which two procedure codes the insurer considers bundled.

**Step 2:** Look up the code pair in the CMS NCCI edits table, published publicly at [cms.gov](https://www.cms.gov). Search for the two CPT codes to see whether they are officially bundled — and whether a modifier can override the bundling rule.

**Step 3:** Ask your provider's billing department. They should know whether the services were distinct and whether a modifier should have been applied. If it was a billing error on their side, a corrected claim is faster than a formal dispute.

## How to Fix a CO-97 Denial: Step-by-Step

### Step 1: Call your provider's billing department

Explain that you received a CO-97 denial and ask them to review the claim. Many CO-97 issues are resolved with a corrected claim or resubmission with the appropriate modifier — no formal dispute required on your end.

### Step 2: Request the clinical documentation

If the services were clinically distinct — separate procedures, separate sessions, or separately documented in your chart — ask your provider to confirm this in writing. This documentation supports a modifier-based resubmission.

### Step 3: File a dispute if the denial was wrong

If the services were legitimately separate and a corrected claim doesn't resolve it, file a formal dispute with your insurer. Your dispute should:

- State that the two procedures were distinct and not bundled under the applicable NCCI edit (or cite the modifier exception that applies)
- Include clinical notes documenting both procedures separately
- Request the specific bundling rule or NCCI edit the insurer applied

### Step 4: Submit before the deadline

Check your EOB for the dispute filing window. Most plans require disputes within 180 days of the denial date, though deadlines vary by state and plan type.

## When to Resubmit vs. When to File a Formal Dispute

**Resubmit a corrected claim if:** Your provider's billing team missed the correct modifier (-59, -XU, -25, or similar) or used an incorrect procedure code. A corrected claim is faster and simpler than a formal dispute.

**File a formal dispute if:** The modifier was applied correctly, the services were genuinely distinct, and the insurer still denied. You'll need clinical documentation and a written argument against the bundling determination.

## Your Rights as a Patient

You are entitled to a detailed written explanation of why your claim was denied, including the specific bundling rule or NCCI edit the insurer applied. If you request this and don't receive a clear answer, your state insurance commissioner may be able to help.

Under the ACA, all non-grandfathered health plans must provide internal appeal rights for denied claims. If internal appeals fail, external review is available for certain dispute types.

For Medicare patients: CO-97 disputes can be filed through the Medicare appeals process, which has five levels. The Medicare Rights Center at [medicarerights.org](https://www.medicarerights.org) offers free guidance on Medicare-specific billing disputes.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**What's the difference between CO-97 and CO-50?**
CO-50 is a clinical denial — your insurer says the treatment wasn't medically necessary. CO-97 is a billing dispute — one service was considered already included in a payment for another. CO-97 almost always involves a billing code or modifier issue, not a question of whether you needed the care.

**Will my provider handle the CO-97 dispute for me?**
Often yes. Providers have billing departments that handle these regularly. It's worth asking them to review and resubmit before you file your own dispute — it's faster and they understand the code-level details.

**What is a modifier, and do I need to understand it?**
A modifier is a two-digit code your provider attaches to a procedure code to signal that the service was distinct from another billed service. You don't need to know the technical details — but knowing that modifiers exist can help you have a more productive conversation with your provider's billing team.

**Can I dispute a CO-97 if I've already paid my bill?**
Yes. If you paid out-of-pocket for a service that should have been covered, you can still file a claim dispute or request reimbursement through your insurer. Time limits apply — check your plan documents for the specific window.

**What if both the provider and insurer agree the service is bundled?**
If CMS NCCI edits clearly bundle the two services and no modifier exception applies, the denial may be correct. The question then is whether your provider should have billed them separately — a conversation to have directly with your provider.
