# CO-22 Denial Code: Coordination of Benefits — What It Means When You Have Two Insurance Plans

Getting a CO-22 denial code on your insurance claim can be confusing, especially when you thought you had coverage. Here is what you need to know: fewer than 1% of patients ever appeal an insurance denial, even though 40–60% of those who do end up winning, according to KFF analysis of Healthcare.gov data through 2023. A CO-22 denial is almost always an administrative issue related to how your insurance plans communicate with each other — and it is one of the most fixable denials in medical billing.

CO-22 means "This care may be covered by another payer per coordination of benefits." In plain language, your insurance company is saying it believes another insurance plan should pay first, or that it needs more information about your other coverage before it can process the claim.

## What Is Coordination of Benefits and Why Does It Matter?

Coordination of benefits — often shortened to COB — is the process insurers use to determine which plan pays first when a patient has coverage under more than one health insurance policy. This is more common than many people realize. You might have dual coverage if you are on your own employer's plan and also covered as a dependent on a spouse's plan, if you have Medicare plus a supplemental or employer plan, if you are a dependent child covered by both parents, or if you have Medicaid as secondary coverage alongside a private plan.

When you have two plans, one is designated as "primary" (pays first) and the other as "secondary" (pays the remaining balance, up to its coverage limits). The rules for determining which plan is primary follow a standard order established by the National Association of Insurance Commissioners. Generally, your own employer plan is primary over a spouse's plan, and for children, the "birthday rule" applies — the parent whose birthday falls earlier in the calendar year has the primary plan.

A CO-22 denial happens when your insurer either does not know about your other coverage, has incorrect information about which plan is primary, or needs confirmation from the other insurer before processing.

## Most Common Causes of a CO-22 Denial

The single most common cause is outdated or missing COB information on file with your insurer. When you enrolled or last updated your information, you may not have reported your other coverage — or the information may have changed since then. Insurance companies periodically send COB questionnaires to verify whether you have other coverage, and if you do not respond, they may default to denying claims until the information is confirmed.

Another frequent cause is incorrect primary/secondary designation. If both insurers believe the other one should pay first, neither will process the claim, and you will receive denials from both. This sometimes happens after a life event like a marriage, divorce, or job change when COB records have not been updated on all plans.

Claims can also be denied with CO-22 when your provider submits the claim to the wrong insurer first. If your provider bills your secondary plan before the primary plan has processed the claim, the secondary plan will deny it and direct you back to the primary.

Finally, system errors between insurers can trigger CO-22 denials. The electronic data interchange systems that insurers use to communicate COB information are not perfect, and records can become out of sync.

## How to Fix a CO-22 Denial: Step by Step

**Step 1: Determine your primary and secondary coverage.** Call both insurance companies and confirm which plan is designated as primary. If you are unsure, provide both insurers with the other plan's group number, member ID, and effective date. They will apply standard COB rules to determine the order.

**Step 2: Update your COB information.** Contact the insurer that issued the CO-22 denial and update your coordination of benefits records. You may need to fill out a COB questionnaire — ask them to send one, or check if you can complete it online through your member portal. Be sure to update both insurers so their records match.

**Step 3: Ensure the claim goes to the primary insurer first.** If the claim was sent to the wrong plan first, contact your provider's billing department and ask them to submit the claim to your primary insurer. Once the primary plan processes the claim and issues an EOB, the remaining balance can be submitted to the secondary plan along with the primary plan's EOB.

**Step 4: Request reprocessing.** Once your COB information is updated, call the insurer that denied the claim and ask them to reprocess it. Provide your updated COB details and reference the original claim number. Many insurers can initiate reprocessing over the phone, but some may require a written request.

**Step 5: Follow up on both plans.** Mark your calendar to check the status of the claim with both your primary and secondary insurers within 30 days. Confirm that the primary plan has processed and paid its portion, and that the secondary plan has received and is processing the remaining balance.

## When You Need a Formal Appeal

Most CO-22 denials are resolved by simply updating your COB information and requesting reprocessing. You typically do not need to file a formal appeal for this type of denial.

However, a formal appeal may be necessary if your insurer refuses to reprocess after you have updated your COB information, if there is a dispute between your two insurers about which is primary that you cannot resolve by phone, or if you have already confirmed you only have one insurance plan and the insurer will not accept that information.

If you need to escalate, your state insurance commissioner's office can help mediate disputes between insurers. You can find your state commissioner through the National Association of Insurance Commissioners directory at [naic.org](https://www.naic.org). CMS also provides guidance on COB rules for Medicare-related disputes at [cms.gov](https://www.cms.gov).

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Can I be billed while a CO-22 denial is being resolved?

Your provider should not send you to collections while a COB issue is being worked out. If you receive a bill, contact the billing department and explain that the claim is pending reprocessing due to a coordination of benefits update. Ask them to place a hold on the balance. Keep a record of this conversation.

### What if I only have one insurance plan but keep getting CO-22 denials?

This usually means your insurer has stale COB data suggesting you have other coverage. Call member services and explicitly confirm that you have only one active plan. Ask them to update your COB record to reflect "no other coverage" and request that the claim be reprocessed. If they require it in writing, send a brief letter stating that you do not have secondary coverage, along with your member ID and the denied claim number.

### How long does it take to resolve a CO-22 denial?

Once your COB information is updated, reprocessing typically takes 14 to 30 days. If both insurers need to coordinate, the full resolution may take 30 to 60 days. Keeping records of every call — including the representative's name and any reference numbers — will help you follow up efficiently.

### Does CO-22 affect my coverage or benefits?

No. A CO-22 denial does not mean your services are not covered. It is purely an administrative flag about the order in which your insurance plans should pay. Once the COB issue is resolved, the claim should be processed according to your normal plan benefits.
