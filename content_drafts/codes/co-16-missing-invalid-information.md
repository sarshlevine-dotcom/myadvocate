# CO-16 Denial Code: Missing or Invalid Information — What It Means and How to Fix It

You are not alone. About 1 in 5 insurance claims are denied, and the CO-16 code — "missing or invalid information" — is the single most common reason. It accounts for roughly 15–20% of all insurance denials, according to KFF analysis of Healthcare.gov data through 2023. The good news: this denial is almost always fixable, and it typically doesn't require a formal appeal.

---

## What Does CO-16 Mean?

**CO-16** stands for Claim Adjustment Reason Code 16. It means the insurance company could not process your claim because required information was missing, incomplete, or incorrect on the claim submission.

This is an administrative denial — not a decision that your service isn't covered or isn't medically necessary. The insurer isn't saying no to your claim. They're saying they need more information before they can process it.

**Plain language:** Your claim was missing something the insurer needs. Fix the paperwork, and most CO-16 denials get processed.

---

## Common Reasons a Claim Gets a CO-16 Denial

The specific missing information varies, but these are the most frequent causes:

- **Missing patient information** — name, date of birth, or member ID was incorrect or missing
- **Invalid or missing diagnosis code (ICD-10)** — the diagnosis code was left blank, used the wrong format, or doesn't match the service billed
- **Invalid procedure code (CPT)** — the code used doesn't exist, was discontinued, or requires a modifier that wasn't included
- **Missing provider information** — the billing provider's NPI number or Tax ID was missing or invalid
- **Missing or expired referral** — some plans require a referral from a primary care physician before seeing a specialist
- **Missing authorization number** — if the service required prior authorization, the authorization number must appear on the claim
- **Incomplete coordination of benefits information** — if you have more than one insurance plan, the primary/secondary payer order may not have been established

The denial letter or Explanation of Benefits (EOB) should include a remark code alongside CO-16 that identifies which specific piece of information is missing. Common remark codes include:
- **MA130** — missing or invalid information
- **N30** — patient cannot be identified as the insurer's insured
- **N290** — missing or invalid rendering provider information

If your denial includes a remark code, look that code up to get the exact field that needs correcting. The CMS publishes the full list of Claim Adjustment Reason Codes at [cms.gov/medicare/coding-billing/claim-adjustment-reason-codes](https://www.cms.gov/medicare/coding-billing/claim-adjustment-reason-codes).

## How to Fix a CO-16 Denial

A CO-16 denial doesn't go through the standard appeal process. It goes through a **corrected claim resubmission**. Here's the path:

### Step 1 — Get the Details

Call your provider's billing office and tell them the claim was denied with a CO-16 code. Ask them:
- Which specific field was missing or invalid
- Whether the remark code gives more detail
- Whether they have the original claim on file

### Step 2 — Identify the Missing Information

If the issue is with patient information or insurance ID numbers, you may need to confirm your current insurance information with your provider. Bring your insurance card to the next appointment and ask the front desk to verify your information on file.

If the issue is with a referral or authorization, confirm with your doctor's office whether one was required and whether it was obtained.

### Step 3 — Submit a Corrected Claim

Your provider's billing office submits a corrected claim (using claim frequency type code 7 on a CMS-1500 form, or the equivalent for your billing format) with the missing or corrected information included. This is a standard billing function — your provider has done it before.

You typically don't submit this yourself. Your provider submits it. Your role is to ensure they have everything they need and to follow up to confirm it was submitted.

### Step 4 — Confirm Processing

After the corrected claim is submitted, allow 30 days for processing. Follow up with your provider's billing office to confirm it was received and check the status. If the corrected claim is denied again with a different code, the problem has shifted — follow the guidance for that new code.

## When You Need a Formal Dispute Instead

Most CO-16 denials resolve with a corrected resubmission. However, you may need a formal dispute letter if:

- The provider insists all information was correct, but the insurer still denies with CO-16
- The same claim has been denied with CO-16 multiple times after corrections
- The insurer's EOB is not providing enough information to identify what's missing

In that case, the [Medical Bill Dispute tool](/how-to-dispute-medical-bill) can generate a formal dispute letter requesting the insurer specify exactly what information is required and provide an itemized explanation of the denial.

## CO-16 and Other Administrative Denials

CO-16 is the most common code in the administrative denial category, which covers paperwork, billing, and documentation errors. Understanding the full category helps if your denial code changes after resubmission:

- **[CO-97](/denial-codes/co-97-bundled-included-service)** — the service was considered bundled into another code already billed
- **CO-18** — duplicate claim (the same claim was submitted twice)
- **CO-22** — coordination of benefits issue between multiple insurance plans
- **CO-29** — timely filing deadline passed

All of these are in the same administrative category. Most resolve without a formal appeal. See the full [Administrative Denial Category](/insurance-denial/administrative-denial) for a complete guide.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Do I need to file a formal appeal for a CO-16 denial?**
Usually no. A CO-16 denial is a paperwork issue, not a coverage decision. The standard fix is a corrected claim resubmission by your provider's billing office. A formal appeal is only necessary if the corrected claim continues to be denied.

**How long do I have to fix a CO-16 denial?**
Your insurer has a timely filing deadline — the window within which a corrected claim must be submitted. This is typically 90 to 180 days from the original date of service, though it varies by plan. Your provider's billing office should know the deadline for your specific insurer. Don't wait.

**Can I get help if my provider won't resubmit the corrected claim?**
If your provider's billing office is unresponsive, contact your insurer's member services line and ask them directly what specific information is needed. Document the call. Then ask your provider in writing to submit the corrected claim with those specific corrections.

**Will a CO-16 denial affect my credit?**
A denied insurance claim, on its own, doesn't go to collections. However, if the claim is never corrected and the provider eventually bills you directly, and you don't pay that bill, it can be sent to collections. Act on the denial promptly to prevent this path.
