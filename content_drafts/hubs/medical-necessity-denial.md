# Medical Necessity Denials: How to Appeal When Insurance Says No

If your insurance company denied a claim labeled "not medically necessary," you are not alone — and you are not out of options. Fewer than 1% of patients ever appeal an insurance denial, even though 40–60% of those who do, win, according to KFF analysis of Healthcare.gov data through 2023. Medical necessity denials are among the most commonly overturned on appeal. Your doctor said you needed the care. Now it's time to make your insurer listen.

This guide explains what a medical necessity denial really means, why insurers issue them, and exactly how to appeal one — step by step.

---

## What "Not Medically Necessary" Actually Means

When an insurer denies a claim as "not medically necessary," they are not saying your treatment didn't happen or that your doctor was wrong. What they're saying is that, based on their review — often a desk review by someone who has never examined you — the treatment didn't meet their criteria for coverage.

Insurance companies use internal clinical criteria (often derived from guidelines published by organizations like InterQual or MCG Health) to define what they consider "medically necessary." These criteria are not always the same as what your doctor, your specialist, or evidence-based clinical guidelines would recommend.

The gap between what your doctor says you need and what your insurer agrees to cover is exactly where appeals succeed.

---

## Common Reasons Insurers Deny on Medical Necessity Grounds

**The treatment is considered experimental or investigational.**
If a procedure or medication is relatively new, an insurer may classify it as experimental — even if your doctor considers it standard of care for your condition.

**The insurer says a less expensive treatment should come first.**
This is sometimes called a "step therapy" or "fail first" requirement. The insurer may require you to try (and fail) a cheaper alternative before approving a more targeted treatment.

**The documentation didn't support the level of care requested.**
Insurers review clinical notes, test results, and physician documentation. If the notes are vague, incomplete, or don't clearly connect your diagnosis to the requested service, the reviewer may deny the claim.

**The service exceeds frequency or duration guidelines.**
Some plans limit how often a service can be used — for example, a certain number of physical therapy sessions per year. A request above that threshold may trigger a medical necessity review.

**The requested service doesn't match the diagnosis code.**
If the procedure code doesn't align with the diagnosis code in the insurer's criteria, the claim may be automatically denied before a human ever reviews it.

## Your Right to Appeal

Under the Affordable Care Act, you have the right to appeal any insurance denial — including medical necessity denials — through at least two levels: an internal appeal with your insurer and an external review by an independent organization. This applies to most marketplace plans, employer-sponsored plans, and many other health insurance types.

For Medicare beneficiaries, a separate five-level appeals process applies through the [CMS Medicare Appeals program](https://www.cms.gov/medicare/appeals-grievances).

State laws may provide additional protections. Your state's insurance commissioner is a resource if your insurer is not responding fairly. You can find your commissioner through the [NAIC state directory](https://www.naic.org/state_web_map.htm).

## Step-by-Step: How to Appeal a Medical Necessity Denial

### Step 1 — Request the specific denial reason in writing

Call the member services number on the back of your insurance card. Ask for:

- The exact clinical criteria used to evaluate the claim
- The name and credentials of the reviewer who made the denial decision
- The full denial letter if you haven't already received one

You have the right to this information under federal law.

### Step 2 — Ask your doctor to write a letter of medical necessity

This is often the single most important step. Your doctor's letter should:

- State clearly why the treatment is necessary for your specific condition
- Reference your diagnosis (with the ICD-10 code if possible)
- Cite relevant clinical guidelines, peer-reviewed studies, or professional society recommendations that support the treatment
- Explain why alternatives are inappropriate or have already failed

A strong letter of medical necessity, written by a physician who knows your case, is the foundation of a winning appeal.

### Step 3 — Review your plan's clinical criteria

You have the right to receive the specific criteria your insurer used to deny your claim. Compare those criteria to your doctor's documentation. Look for gaps — places where the documentation may not have used the exact language the insurer's criteria requires.

### Step 4 — Write your appeal letter

Your appeal letter should:

- Reference your claim number, date of service, and the denial code
- Quote the insurer's stated reason for denial
- Explain point by point why the denial is incorrect
- Attach your doctor's letter of medical necessity and any supporting clinical evidence
- Cite your plan's coverage language if it supports your position

Keep the tone factual and specific. Vague appeals are easy to deny again.

### Step 5 — Submit within the deadline

Most plans require internal appeals within 180 days of the denial notice. Check your plan's Summary of Benefits and Coverage (SBC) for your exact deadline. Missing the deadline can forfeit your appeal rights.

### Step 6 — Request an external review if needed

If your internal appeal is denied, you can request an external review — an independent, legally binding evaluation by a third party. Insurers must comply with the external reviewer's decision. The [HHS Office of Inspector General](https://oig.hhs.gov) provides guidance on external review rights by plan type.

## What Evidence Strengthens a Medical Necessity Appeal

The strongest appeals include:

- **Physician letter of medical necessity** — specific, detailed, and signed by your treating provider
- **Clinical guidelines** — published standards from professional medical societies (e.g., American College of Physicians, American Cancer Society) that support the treatment
- **Peer-reviewed research** — journal articles showing the treatment's effectiveness for your condition
- **Your treatment history** — documentation showing what you've already tried and why alternatives were ineffective or inappropriate
- **Specialist opinion** — a second specialist opinion can carry significant weight, especially for complex or rare conditions

## Denial Code CO-50: The Most Common Medical Necessity Code

If your denial shows code CO-50, that's the standard adjustment reason code for "not medically necessary." It's used across virtually all commercial insurance plans and Medicare.

A CO-50 denial doesn't mean your appeal is hopeless — it means you need to address the specific criteria your insurer used in their review.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Can my insurer override my doctor's recommendation?**
Yes — insurers can and do deny coverage even when a physician recommends treatment. However, that denial is not final. You have the right to appeal, and your doctor's clinical judgment is a powerful element of any appeal.

**Q: How long does a medical necessity appeal take?**
Standard internal appeals must be decided within 30 to 60 days depending on plan type. Urgent appeals — when your health may be at serious risk — must be decided within 72 hours. Request an expedited appeal if your situation is time-sensitive.

**Q: What if my doctor doesn't want to write a letter?**
Explain to your provider that the appeal requires their clinical judgment and that the letter directly supports your ability to receive covered care. Most physicians will write a letter when they understand how important it is to the patient's case.

**Q: Does appealing cost anything?**
Federal law prohibits insurers from charging fees for appeals. The internal and external review processes are free.

**Q: Can a medical necessity denial affect my future coverage?**
No. Filing an appeal or having a claim denied does not affect your coverage status, your premiums, or your eligibility for future claims. Insurers cannot penalize you for exercising your appeal rights.

**Q: What if the external review upholds the denial?**
An external review denial means the independent reviewer agreed with the insurer's decision. At that point, you may still have options — including state insurance commissioner complaints or, in some cases, consultation with a patient advocate or attorney.
