# Administrative Insurance Denials Explained: Paperwork, Billing, and Documentation Errors

Getting a denial letter in the mail can feel like a gut punch—especially when your care was real and your need was real. But fewer than 1% of patients ever appeal an insurance denial, even though 40–60% of those who do, win, according to KFF analysis of Healthcare.gov data through 2023. When the denial is administrative, that win rate tends to be even higher. Administrative denials are about paperwork, not your health—and paperwork can almost always be fixed.

This guide covers the most common types of administrative denials, explains what each one means in plain language, and walks you through what to do next.

---

## What Is an Administrative Insurance Denial?

An administrative denial means your insurance company rejected your claim for a reason that has nothing to do with whether your treatment was medically appropriate. Instead, the denial points to a problem with how the claim was filed, coded, documented, or submitted.

Common triggers include:

- Missing or incorrect information on the claim form
- A billing code that doesn't match the service provided
- A prior authorization that was never obtained (or expired)
- A duplicate claim that looks like you're billing for the same service twice
- A claim filed after the plan's deadline

These are frustrating, but they are fixable. The insurer isn't saying your care wasn't needed—they're saying there was a problem with the paperwork.

---

## The Most Common Administrative Denial Types

### Missing or Invalid Information (CO-16)

If your explanation of benefits (EOB) shows denial code CO-16, the insurer flagged the claim for missing or incorrect information. This might be a wrong date of birth, a missing referring provider number, or a procedure code that doesn't match the diagnosis.

The fix is usually simple: identify the specific field that was rejected, correct it, and resubmit. Your provider's billing department can often handle this directly.

### Timely Filing Limit Exceeded (CO-29)

Every insurance plan has a deadline for submitting claims—often 90 days to one year from the date of service. If the claim wasn't filed in time, you'll see CO-29 on your EOB.

This one is worth fighting if you have evidence that the delay wasn't your fault. Insurance carriers are required to consider documentation showing billing delays caused by system errors, provider onboarding problems, or other extenuating circumstances.

[How to dispute a CO-29 timely filing denial →](/denial-codes/co-29-timely-filing)

---

### Bundled or Included Service (CO-97)

CO-97 means the insurer says one of the services billed was already included in the payment for another procedure. Sometimes that's correct—certain follow-up services are packaged into the original procedure fee. But sometimes it's a coding error on the provider's end, and an unbundling appeal is appropriate.

[How to dispute a CO-97 bundling denial →](/denial-codes/co-97-bundled-included-service)

---

### Duplicate Claim (CO-18)

CO-18 appears when the insurer believes it already received and processed a claim for the same service. This can happen when a provider resubmits a claim that was initially lost, or when two claims with slightly different codes are flagged as duplicates.

If you have confirmation that the original claim was never paid, gather that documentation and submit a dispute with proof of prior non-payment.

[How to dispute a CO-18 duplicate claim →](/denial-codes/co-18-duplicate-claim)

---

### Prior Authorization Not Obtained

Some plans require advance approval—called prior authorization—before certain procedures, specialists, or medications are covered. If authorization wasn't obtained (or wasn't obtained correctly), the claim may be denied.

This type of denial is common in cases involving:

- Elective surgeries or outpatient procedures
- Specialty medications
- Durable medical equipment
- Mental health or substance use treatment above a certain threshold

If your provider failed to get prior auth, they may be able to request a retroactive authorization—especially in urgent or emergency situations. Your plan's member services line can tell you whether that process is available.

---

### Coordination of Benefits (CO-22)

If you're covered by more than one insurance plan—for example, through your employer and your spouse's employer—your insurers need to coordinate which one pays first. CO-22 means there's a conflict in that coordination, and the claim is on hold until it's resolved.

You'll typically need to contact both insurers and confirm which is primary and which is secondary. Once that's documented, claims usually process correctly.

## Step-by-Step: What to Do After an Administrative Denial

**1. Read your Explanation of Benefits (EOB) carefully.**
Your EOB will include the denial code (like CO-16 or CO-29) and a brief description of why the claim was rejected. Write it down.

**2. Call your insurance company.**
Use the member services number on the back of your card. Ask them to explain the denial in plain language and confirm exactly what documentation or correction is needed.

**3. Contact your provider's billing department.**
For most administrative denials, the provider—not the patient—is responsible for resubmitting corrected claims. Share the denial code and ask them to correct and resubmit.

**4. Keep a paper trail.**
Log every call with a date, time, and the name of the representative you spoke with. Request confirmation numbers when available.

**5. File a formal dispute if resubmission fails.**
If the corrected claim is denied again, you have the right to file a formal appeal. Most plans allow at least one internal appeal; if that fails, you can request an external review by an independent organization.

---

## When Does a Simple Fix Become a Full Appeal?

Most administrative denials can be resolved through a corrected resubmission—no formal appeal needed. But a formal dispute letter is appropriate when:

- The insurer continues to deny a corrected claim without new explanation
- The denial is based on a policy interpretation you disagree with (such as what counts as "bundled")
- You have documentation showing the filing delay was caused by factors outside your control
- The insurer is applying a timely filing deadline incorrectly

A well-written dispute letter—one that cites the specific denial code, references your plan's appeal rights, and includes supporting documentation—significantly improves your odds.

---

## Your Rights When an Administrative Denial Feels Wrong

Under the Affordable Care Act, all marketplace and employer-sponsored health plans must provide at least one internal appeals process and access to external, independent review for clinical or coverage disputes. The [CMS](https://www.cms.gov) publishes guidance on appeal rights by plan type.

For Medicare beneficiaries, the [CMS Medicare Appeals](https://www.cms.gov/medicare/appeals-grievances) program outlines a five-level appeals process.

State insurance commissioners can also intervene when an insurer appears to be applying denial rules inconsistently or in bad faith. Find your state's insurance commissioner through the [National Association of Insurance Commissioners (NAIC)](https://www.naic.org/state_web_map.htm).

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: How long do I have to appeal an administrative denial?**
Most plans give you 180 days from the date of the denial notice to file an internal appeal. Check your plan's Summary of Benefits and Coverage (SBC) for your specific deadline.

**Q: Can I be billed for care that was denied for administrative reasons?**
It depends on your plan and the denial type. If your provider failed to get required prior authorization, you may be responsible for the bill—but this varies. Ask your insurer and provider billing department to clarify who bears the cost before paying anything.

**Q: What if my provider refuses to resubmit a corrected claim?**
If your provider won't cooperate, you can still file an appeal directly with your insurer as a patient. Include the denial code, your EOB, and a written explanation of why you believe the denial was in error.

**Q: Does an administrative denial affect my medical history or coverage status?**
No. Administrative denials are billing issues—they don't affect your medical records, your coverage status, or your insurer's obligation to cover future claims.

**Q: What's the difference between a denial and a rejection?**
A rejection means the claim was never processed—usually because of a data entry error. A denial means the claim was processed but not paid. Rejections can typically be corrected and resubmitted more quickly than denials.
